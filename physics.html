The Physics of Noise Colors

In audio engineering, "colored" noise refers to signals where the power spectral density (PSD) is a function of frequency \(f\). These are generally defined by the power-law relationship:

$$S(f) \propto \frac{1}{f^\alpha}$$

White Noise ($\alpha = 0$)

White noise has a flat PSD ($1/f^0$). It contains equal power per unit of bandwidth. To the human ear, it sounds high-pitched because octaves at higher frequencies contain more absolute bandwidth than lower ones.

Pink Noise ($\alpha = 1$)

Pink noise, or $1/f$ noise, has equal power per octave. The power density decreases by 3 dB per octave. This profile is frequently observed in biological systems and natural phenomena.

Brownian Noise ($\alpha = 2$)

Brownian noise (Red noise) decreases by 6 dB per octave ($1/f^2$). It is mathematically the integral of white noise, representing Brownian motion. It is characterized by heavy low-frequency energy.

Grey Noise

Unlike the power-law noises above, Grey noise is defined psychoacoustically. It is noise subjected to an inverted Equal-Loudness Contour (ISO 226), making it sound equally loud to a human listener across all frequencies.
